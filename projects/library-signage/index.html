Library Signage

Submitted: 2nd January 2018

Overview

 

I used the CNC machine and a laser cutter to make an interesting sign for our library at The Making Rooms.

 

NB) This tutorial assumes the user has basic knowledge of how to use Inkscape, the laser cutter, V-Carve (the software, not the type of router bit), and the CNC.

Step 1. Design

Draw out your design using Inkscape and split it into seperate files for the seperate sheets of material and the two machines that you’re going to be using.

Tip) Make sure that the outline of the laser cut areas have no sharp corners in them, nothing lower than a 3mm diameter, as this is the dimentions of the smallest (reasonable) CNC milling bit.

Step 2. Model your design

Cut out your design in paper or card and construct it as a model to make sure that it will fit. For this project I needed to check overall fit and also font size, designed to be seen from afar.

Step 3. Laser cutting part 1

To achieve the effect in the photos remove the protective film from back of the clear acrylic. AKA not the “show face” side. Leave the “show face” protected. Spray paint the back of the acrylic with suitable black spray paint. This black became the text in my project.

Step 4. Laser cutting part 2

Laser etch away the parts that you want to be sprayed in the background colour.

Tip 1) Don’t etch deeply, but prehaps etch the design twice instead. This helps remove residue more cleanly. Absolutle do not open the laser lid between these two etches, tiny moevements of the material will be detrimental.

Tip 2) Slower etching settings will give a very slightly cleaner edge to your design as it is etching as the laser head isnt racing around.

Tip 3) If the background colour touches the edge of your design at any point etch away a ‘bleed margin’ at that point.

Step 5. Laser cutting part 3

Remove the clear acrylic form the laser cutter. You can now spray the background colour over the etched areas.

Step 6. Laser cutting part 4

Once dry, put the clear acrylic back into the laser cutter and cut the signs out. Cutting at the end like this makes sure your edges are kept clean which is important for clear acrylic (dirt on the edges looks magnified with this material).

Keep these pieces very safe while you CNC the main body of the sign, this paint is very easily scratched while it is exposed.

Step 7. CNC milling part 1

Open your .SVG in V-carve and assign pocket cut milling paths to the internal pockets that you want the laser cut signs to sit into.

Assign profile cuts to the outside cutting pathsof your sign.

Tip 1) Keep the outlines on the design file for the laser and the CNC milling machine the same. Don’t outset or inset the cut lines on your drawing. A very tight fit is preferable and means no glue will need to be used which might damage your lovely etch/spraypaint job.

Tip 2) I made the depth of the pocket cuts a litle deeper than the depth of the laser cut material be because I have done this type of project previously and the laser cut part was slighlty proud of the CNC part and it took ages of sanding to get the pocket cut down deep enough.

Step 8. CNC milling part 2

Tab out your work once the CNC is finished and sand carefully.

Step 9. Construction

Use a rubber mallet and a piece of wood (bigger than the little laser cut parts) to bash the laser cut parts into the CNC sign. The wood will prevent undue stress on the little laser cut pieces as arylic can be brittle.

Tip 1) If your pocket cuts aren’t deep enough after all and it looks like your lasered parts will never come out don’t worry, bash on the back of the sign with the rubber mallet and the laser pieces will eventually stat to worm their way out of the pockets.

Step 10. Grand Finale

Peel away that protective film form the “show face” of the clear acrylic.

Voila!

You can now hang your sign.

Materials Required

  • Clear 3mm acrylic
  • 12mm CNC suitable birch plywood
  • Spray paint in your chosen colours

Equipment and Settings

Small Laser Cutter

Settings:

etch: Speed 100%, Power 20%
cut: Speed 35%, Power 100%, Frequency 500Hz

Shopbot CNC Router

Settings:

3mm milling bit - feeds and speeds kept 'as is'
12mm milling bit - feeds and speeds kept 'as is'
